PHASE 01 Foundations & Pencil Control Weeks 1–3 ▾

Build confidence with line quality, basic forms, and the iPad as a drawing tool.

LESSON 01
✓
Lines, Pressure & Gesture Strokes
Get comfortable with Apple Pencil pressure sensitivity and make intentional marks.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Set up Procreate: create a canvas, pick a simple pencil brush
  • Draw 50 straight horizontal lines varying from whisper-light to fully pressed
  • Draw 50 curved lines — smooth arcs, S-curves, spirals
  • Practice "ghosting": hover the pencil along a path before touching down
  • Fill one page with loose, fast gestural marks — no second-guessing
30 min · Every other day × 1 wkFoundations
LESSON 02
✓
Basic Forms: Sphere, Box, Cylinder
Understand how 3D forms are constructed — these become heads, torsos, and limbs later.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Draw a circle → shade one side lightly for a sphere (no reference yet)
  • Draw a box in 3/4 view; sketch the top, front, and side faces
  • Draw a cylinder — ellipse on top, rectangle sides, ellipse at base
  • Combine 3 forms into a simple robot figure (box body, sphere head, cylinders for arms)
  • Rotate each form: draw the same object from 3 different angles
35 min · 3 sessionsFoundations
LESSON 03
✓
Contour Drawing & Observation
Train your eye-hand connection — the core skill all drawing depends on.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Pick a simple object (mug, shoe, phone). Draw its outline without lifting the pencil
  • Do a "blind contour": draw while looking only at the object, not your screen
  • Draw the same object again looking at both — compare the two versions
  • Repeat with 2 other objects; focus on negative space around them
30 min · 2 sessionsFoundations
LESSON 04
✓
Value & Light: Simple Shading
Learn to model form with light and shadow — makes everything feel 3D.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Create a value scale: 6 boxes from white → black using only pencil pressure
  • Draw a sphere; pick a light source from upper-left; shade the shadow side
  • Add a cast shadow beneath the sphere on the ground plane
  • Draw the box from Lesson 02 with light hitting only the top face
  • Try cross-hatching vs. smooth blending — notice the texture difference
40 min · 2 sessionsFoundations
PHASE 02 Perspective Drawing Weeks 4–7 ▾

Master 1-point and 2-point perspective to build convincing environments for your characters.

LESSON 05
✓
1-Point Perspective: The Room
Understand the horizon line, vanishing point, and how depth works on a flat page.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Draw a horizontal line across the canvas middle — this is your horizon
  • Place one dot on the line (vanishing point). Draw a rectangle for the room's back wall
  • Draw lines from the rectangle corners to the VP — these become walls, floor, ceiling
  • Add a door, a window, and a box on the floor using the same vanishing point
  • Sketch the same room from a low horizon (worm's-eye) and high horizon (bird's-eye)
40 min · 3 sessionsPerspective
LESSON 06
✓
2-Point Perspective: City Blocks
Draw buildings and objects at an angle — essential for dynamic scenes.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Draw a horizon line; place two vanishing points near each end
  • Draw a vertical edge in the center — the near corner of a building
  • Draw lines from the top and bottom of this edge to both vanishing points
  • Add a second building behind the first; add windows using the same VPs
  • Draw a simple street scene: two buildings, a sidewalk, a lamp post
40 min · 4 sessionsPerspective
LESSON 07
✓
Objects in Perspective: Furniture & Props
Place everyday objects convincingly in a scene — these will become storytelling props.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Draw a table in 2-point perspective; add a chair beside it
  • Draw a bookshelf against a wall; add books of varying widths
  • Place a cup and lamp on the table — each uses the same vanishing points
  • Sketch a bedroom or café corner using all elements from this lesson
40 min · 3 sessionsPerspective
LESSON 08
✓
Placing a Figure in a Scene
Understand figure scale relative to environments — characters must feel "in" a space.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Key rule: if a figure's head is near the horizon, they're eye-level with the viewer
  • Draw your room from Lesson 05; add a simple stick figure standing in it
  • Move the horizon up — redraw the figure (now you're looking down on them)
  • Add two figures at different depths: far = smaller, close = larger, heads stay near horizon
  • Sketch a café scene with 3 people at different depths
40 min · 3 sessionsPerspective
PHASE 03 Character Construction Weeks 8–12 ▾

Build a repeatable system for drawing human figures, faces, and expressions for storytelling.

LESSON 09
✓
The Head as a 3D Form
Construct a believable head from any angle before adding facial features.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Draw the Loomis method head: a sphere with a flat side plane cut off
  • Add the center line (vertical) and brow line (horizontal) to guide feature placement
  • Rotate the head: draw it facing left, right, and slightly upward
  • Add basic landmarks: brow, eye socket, nose base, chin — no detail yet
  • Do 10 quick head sketches in 10 different rotations on one canvas
45 min · 4 sessionsCharacter
LESSON 10
✓
Faces: Features & Expressions
Draw eyes, nose, and mouth convincingly and use them to convey emotion.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Study the eye as an almond shape; draw 20 eyes at different angles and openness states
  • Draw a simple nose as a wedge — 3 spheres (nostrils + tip) works well for stylized art
  • Draw a mouth: top lip has an M-shape, bottom is fuller. Draw 15 mouths
  • Combine features on your Lesson 09 heads: neutral, happy, angry, sad, surprised
  • Create an emotion chart: 9 expressions on one page for a single character
45 min · 4 sessionsCharacter
LESSON 11
✓
Body Proportions & the Figure
Use the "head as unit" system to draw proportional figures quickly.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Standard: the body is ~7–8 heads tall. Draw a stack of head-sized boxes as a guide
  • Sketch the figure over your stack: head (1), torso (2–4), legs (4–7), feet at 7–8
  • Use simple shapes: egg for ribcage, oval for pelvis, cylinders for limbs
  • Draw the same figure: standing, sitting, crouching
  • Do 5-minute gesture drawings — set a timer, capture just the "flow" of a pose
45 min · 5 sessionsCharacter
LESSON 12
✓
Hands & Expressive Poses
Master the hardest parts of figure drawing; use pose to tell micro-stories.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Draw the hand as a block (palm) + 4 sausages (fingers) + a thumb. Forget detail first
  • Practice 10 hands: pointing, open palm, holding an object, gripping, waving
  • Draw a full figure in a story pose: reading, running, reaching, looking up in awe
  • Use contrapposto (weight on one leg, hip tilt) to make standing poses dynamic, not stiff
45 min · 4 sessionsCharacter
LESSON 13
✓
Your Character: Design & Silhouette
Create an original recurring character for your visual stories.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Design a character silhouette: it should read clearly in solid black with no detail
  • Decide on proportions: realistic (7–8 heads), manga (6–7), or chibi (2–3)
  • Draw a character reference sheet: front, side, 3/4 view, and close-up face
  • Draw them in 3 costumes or outfits that reflect their personality
  • Draw them doing 5 different actions from Lesson 12
45 min · 4 sessionsCharacter
PHASE 04 Visual Storytelling Weeks 13–16 ▾

Bring perspective and character together into composed illustrations and sequential panels.

LESSON 14
✓
Composition: Rule of Thirds & Framing
Make every illustration feel intentional and visually guided.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Draw a 3×3 grid — place your subject at an intersection point, not the center
  • Sketch the same simple scene 4 times with different compositions; compare which reads best
  • Use "lead room": a character looking left should have more space to their left
  • Try framing: use a doorway, tree, or window to frame your subject within the scene
  • Study one favorite illustration: identify where the rule of thirds applies
40 min · 2 sessionsStorytelling
LESSON 15
✓
Camera Angles & Cinematic Shots
Use horizon height like a film director to control emotional impact.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Low angle (horizon below subject): makes character feel powerful or threatening
  • High angle (horizon above): makes character feel small, vulnerable, or observed
  • Eye level: neutral, naturalistic — good for dialogue scenes
  • Draw your character from all 3 angles in the same environment
  • Thumbnail 6 panels of a wordless scene: a character finding something unexpected
45 min · 3 sessionsStorytelling
LESSON 16
✓
Your First Illustration Page
Produce a finished, composed illustration combining everything learned.
▶ Show practice steps
  • Choose a moment: a character arriving somewhere new, discovering an object, or meeting someone
  • Thumbnail 3 compositions (small, rough, 5 min each) — pick the strongest
  • Build it at full canvas size with a perspective grid underneath
  • Sketch the environment first (Phase 2), then place your character (Phase 3)
  • Add value/shading (Phase 1) and refine line weight: thick outlines, thin interior details
  • Optional: add a second panel showing what happens next

App Setup

Use Procreate for its layer system and brush quality. Enable Perspective Drawing Guide via Canvas → Drawing Guide → 2-Point Perspective. It autosnaps lines to your vanishing points.

Layer Strategy

Keep rough sketches on one layer at 30% opacity, then trace clean lines on a layer above. This separates thinking from rendering — a professional habit worth building from day one.

Reference Use

Use Procreate's Reference window or split-screen with Pinterest. Copying from reference is not cheating — it's how every artist trains. The goal is internalizing forms, not memorizing them.
